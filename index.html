Research Activities

Bio-Analysis

Basically, I do analysis of biological data (sequences, SNP genotyping, genetic markers) for vegetable crops. I routinely use bioinformatic softwares (e.g. BLAST, EMBOSS, GBrowse, ...) and scripting tools (Bash, Perl, SQL) to link genetic and genomic data. I also keep an eye out for emerging sequencing and genotyping technologies and for new software solutions.

Transposable elements

For several years, transposable elements were the main focus of my research interests. Transposable elements, or transposons, are discrete genetic elements capable of moving from one genomic location to another. They can be found in nearly all eukaryotic genomes and their mobilization are the cause of spontaneous mutations. They are thus thought to be important players in evolution due to their capacity to generate genetic variability. I am particularly interested in the significance of transposon activation by environmental factors and how these may affect the organization and evolution of genes and genomes. In other words, I am looking for the meaning of life in light of mobile genetic elements.

Transposons in Arabidopsis (my thesis project)

I began studying mobile genetic elements in the course of my Ph.D. project, which I completed under the supervision of Dr. Thomas Bureau at McGill University. Using computer-assisted methods, I participated in a survey to identify transposons in the small plant model system Arabidopsis thaliana,, for which the complete genome sequence is now available. Although Arabidopsis contains relatively few transposons compared to other plants such as maize or barley, it nonetheless harbors a very diverse number of elements. Some of the transposons identified were known to be present, some revealed to be quite novel, and some gave us a better unserstanding of their origin and evolution.

I later used the data obtained from the genome survey to experimentally address the question of transposon mobility and regulation of transposition activity. By adapting an AFLP-derived technique to detect mobility, I tested whether stress factors such as transformation, protoplast isolation and UV-B irradiation can activate some of the transposons identified. Furthermore, I looked into transposon activity in mutants defective for genes known to repress transposition. In addition to identifying potentially active transposons, I also investigated the relationship between environmental signals and transposon activation.

Effect on host genes

Transposon insertions within or near gene coding sequences can affect the expression of host genes at the target site (e.g. frame-shifts, altered expression levels). In effect, these mutations may lead to phenotypic changes that impact the host's fitness. In my current project with Dr. Jean-Marc Deragon, I worked on building a web-interface to a database of transposon-gene associations in Arabidopsis and I selected specific cases to study if genic insertions result in modifications of the plant's phenotype.

Tnt retrotransposons

Tnt1 is a retrotransposon endogenous to tobacco and one of the few known elements that have been shown to be currently active in plants. Demonstration that activation of Tnt1 can be induced by certain types of stress factors has lent support to the hypothesis that, in populations undergoing adverse conditions, transposition can be an important source of genetic variability upon which selection can act. In Dr. Marie-Angèle Grandbastien's team at the INRA, I was investigating the regulation of Tnt1 mobility. I also studied how Tnt1 movement can impact the organization and evolution of the tobacco genome.

Other Projects

Plant virus resistance

From December 2004 to june 2006, I worked on a project involving the RTM resistance genes in the Plant Virus Interactions group at the INRA in Bordeaux. In Arabidopsis, Restricted TEV Movement (RTM) genes confer resistance to the long distance movement of potyviruses such as TEV (Tobacco Etch Virus) and LMV (Lettuce Mosaic Virus). My main objective is to identify the gene for RTM3 by map-based cloning in Arabidopsis. I am also studying the allelic diversity of RTM1 and RTM2 genes in natural accessions (ecotypes) of this plant. By taking advantage of the natural variability of RTM alleles in accessions of Arabidopsis, the aim is to better understand the interactions between the molecular components of the host and those of the pathogen by correlating genetic variability to the phenotypic diversity of the plant host.

Molecular clocks

For my M.Sc. degree, I studied the circadian rhythms in Gonyaulax polyedra. Specifically, I looked at the expression patterns of a protein involved in photosynthesis. I also attempted to establish a protocol for the genetic transformation of G. polyedra usng a biolistic strategy. This work was carried out under the supervision of Dr. David Morse at the Institut de Recherche en Biologie Végétale (Université de Montréal, Canada).

Growth hormones

My first research (undergraduate) project: cloning of TGF-beta type II receptor for GST purification. Research in Dr. Maureen O'Connor's lab at the Biotechnology Research Institute (Canadian National Research Council).
